Cover letter

Hi there, Duckers (Ducks, DuckDuckGoers??) 🦆

My name is Schalk Neethling, and I am applying for the role of Senior Frontend Engineer, React/TypeScript at DuckDuckGo.

Why me?

The projects on your team page are the projects I have done before.

When I read about the work I might be doing — Instant Answers (tools!! — I love building tools) and in-app onboarding — I knew this position was for me.

At Mozilla, I led the frontend engineering for the Firefox onboarding experiment, architected as a WebExtension so that we could ship and A/B test new onboarding flows independently of the browser release cycle. Together with the growth and UX teams, we designed a gamified five-day journey, built telemetry rigorous enough for statistical decisions, and deployed via Funnelcake to cohorts of forty-thousand-plus users.

The experiment achieved an eighty per cent OKR score and was permanently integrated into Firefox for users worldwide. That project demanded everything this role asks for: pragmatic, performant React running consistently across browsers; cross-team collaboration with growth, UX, browser engineering, and release management; success criteria defined upfront and validated against telemetry; and an architecture that respected user privacy by design.

Beyond Firefox onboarding, I led the frontend transition of MDN Web Docs from a Django-based wiki to a React-rendered platform (Yari) — large-scale architecture work for a developer audience of millions, where semantic HTML, accessibility, and performance were treated as first-class concerns throughout.

What I bring that is harder to find

Web-platform-first, with React used deliberately.

I am web-platform-first. Seventeen years of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript experience means I reach for the native primitive before the framework abstraction. When writing React, I use it deliberately: to add what is genuinely useful, to fill in the gaps the platform does not yet cover, and to bridge browsers that are still catching up.

This results in code that is considered, performant, and easy to maintain and understand.

At Factorial, I lead AI integration across our engineering workflows and translate that practice into our client work, working daily with Claude Code, Cursor, Codex, MCP, and Agent Skills in a spec- and test-driven approach.

I am also endlessly curious. As a result, I am building, open sourcing, and releasing a corpus of projects (more on this later) aimed at improving developer workflows and tight feedback loops, to get the best out of AI models and coding agent harnesses.

I was one of the technical reviewers for the latest editions of the O'Reilly JavaScript Definitive Guide, CSS Definitive Guide, Web API Cookbook, and PHP, MySQL, and JavaScript. This work has sharpened both my editorial eye and my standards depth.

I believe this new age of agentic engineering should push us toward better, more accessible, more performant code — and out of our comfort zones. So I have undertaken a large side project, partly for the challenge and partly because it lets me explore something that has fascinated me for years: how does a browser work internally? To answer that, I am building an HTML rendering engine in TypeScript. I know, wild. But that is what this new world of engineering opens us up to. To dream big, and then realise those dreams.

Why DuckDuckGo?

Simple really. Your personal data should be nobody's business.

That sentence has lived rent-free in my head since I first heard it.

I have spent my career advocating for an open, accessible, standards-respecting web, and DuckDuckGo is one of the few large-scale consumer products genuinely fighting that fight while also championing user privacy.

The chance to write code that materially protects millions of people is rare, and it is exactly the kind of meaningful work I want my next chapter to be about.

It is about more than code. It is about being part of something meaningful. ✌️
